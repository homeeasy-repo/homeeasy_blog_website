The Market

Wrigley Field. Boystown. The lakefront. Lakeview is one of Chicago's most residential neighborhoods — and at $3.98/sqft, it undercuts every downtown neighborhood while giving you lake access and the Red Line.

We track 213 buildings in Lakeview — 186 of which are in our portfolio directly. That's the highest building count of any Chicago neighborhood we cover. Competition is real here, just at a different price tier than downtown.

Rent starts at $1,205 and runs up to $3,088 on the starting side, with some units pushing past $7,600. The building stock is mostly vintage — 1950s through 1970s — with a few newer additions like Wrigleyville Lofts (2021). That means character: hardwood floors, bigger layouts, the kind of square footage that new construction can't match at this price.

The Trade-Offs

What You Get

Real neighborhood energy — people walking dogs, local bars, corner grocery stores. Lakefront access. The Red Line to downtown. Lower price per square foot than any downtown neighborhood. Vintage buildings with bigger layouts. Wrigley Field. Boystown. 186 buildings — the most in any Chicago neighborhood. An entry point of $1,205 that's hard to beat.

What You Give Up

You're not downtown. The L ride to the Loop is 15-20 minutes minimum. Nightlife is neighborhood bars and Wrigleyville sports pubs, not River North bottle service. If you need to be in the center of everything, this isn't it. The building stock skews older — don't expect floor-to-ceiling glass and rooftop pools at these prices.

What's Happening in the Market

Lakeview's building stock tells a story. Many of these buildings went up between 1951 and 1970. They're established, they're paid off, and they're competing for tenants against each other and against the shiny new construction downtown. With 186 buildings in our portfolio, the competition drives concessions.

The newer additions are interesting. Wrigleyville Lofts (2021) brought modern construction to the neighborhood at $3.45/sqft — still below the downtown median. That's the market telling you something: even new builds in Lakeview price below what vintage buildings charge in the West Loop.

The $1,205 entry point is one of the lowest starting rents in the portfolio for a neighborhood with lake access and direct L service. If you're budget-constrained but want to stay on the North Side with real transit, this is where the math works.

What Your Budget Gets You Here

Under $1,500/mo: Entry-level units starting at $1,205. You're in Lakeview with lake access and L service. The buildings are older, the finishes are basic. But you're in the neighborhood.

$1,500–$2,000/mo: This is where Lakeview starts to shine. 515 W. Barry at $1,846 is solid value — $3.13/sqft for a lakefront-adjacent building with active concessions. Vintage construction, bigger rooms than anything you'll get downtown at this price.

$2,000–$2,500/mo: Your options open up. Wrigleyville Lofts ($2,171, built 2021) gives you new construction. Reside on Barry ($2,254) puts you in the heart of East Lakeview. Both running concessions.

$2,500+: 441 W. Barry ($2,661) and 3130 N. Lake Shore Dr ($3,088) — the latter puts you on the lakefront in a classic high-rise. At this price in Lakeview, you're getting space and location that would cost significantly more in Lincoln Park or downtown.

Getting Around

The Red Line is the lifeline. Belmont, Addison, Sheridan — multiple stations mean you're never more than a few blocks from a train. The Brown Line runs along the western edge. Downtown in 15-20 minutes. Not 5, not 10. Be honest with yourself about whether that commute works for you.

The Lakefront Trail is right there — runners, cyclists, and dog walkers are on it year-round. Clark Street and Broadway are the commercial spines: groceries, coffee, restaurants, bars. This is a neighborhood where you can do most of your life on foot.

Who Does Well Here

People who want a neighborhood that actually feels like a neighborhood. Not a downtown high-rise canyon, not a suburban quiet — somewhere in between with its own identity. Cubs fans. The LGBTQ+ community, especially around Boystown. Lakefront runners. People who prioritize space and character over being in the center of the action.

If you need to be out in River North three nights a week, this is a long cab ride. If you want to walk to the lake, grab a beer at a local spot, and pay less per square foot than every downtown neighborhood in Chicago, Lakeview is it.
